================================================================================

The **Statement of Interest of the United States** was filed on July 6, 2015, in the case of **Janet F. Bell, et al. v. City of Boise, et al.**, Civil Action No. 1:09-cv-540-REB, in the United States District Court for the District of Idaho. The document is authored by various officials from the U.S. Department of Justice, including Vanita Gupta, Principal Deputy Assistant Attorney General, and Wendy Olson, U.S. Attorney for the District of Idaho, among others [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00000].

The United States highlights the significant issue of homelessness in the U.S., noting that approximately half a million individuals experience homelessness on any given night, with a diverse demographic including children, families, veterans, and the elderly. The causes of homelessness are complex, often linked to economic downturns, health issues, and circumstances beyond individual control, such as domestic violence and trafficking [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00001].

In this case, the plaintiffs, who are homeless individuals, challenge the enforcement of city ordinances that prohibit camping and sleeping in public spaces. They argue that such enforcement violates their Eighth Amendment rights due to inadequate shelter space in Boise. The plaintiffs contend that criminalizing public sleeping in a city with insufficient shelter constitutes the criminalization of homelessness itself [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00001].

The parties disagree on the legal framework for analyzing these claims. The plaintiffs reference **Jones v. City of Los Angeles**, which held that enforcing anti-camping ordinances may violate the Eighth Amendment when adequate shelter is unavailable. Conversely, the defendants argue that the plaintiffs' reliance on **Jones** is misplaced and that there has never been a time when a homeless individual was turned away from a shelter due to lack of space [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00001].

The United States asserts that the **Jones** framework is appropriate for evaluating the plaintiffs' claims. It emphasizes that if sufficient shelter space is unavailable, it becomes impossible for homeless individuals to comply with the ordinances, thereby criminalizing their status as homeless [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00002].

The U.S. government has a vested interest in this case, as it seeks to ensure justice is fairly applied, particularly to vulnerable populations. The **Office for Access to Justice** was established to address access-to-justice issues, and the **U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness** coordinates federal efforts to combat homelessness [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00002].

The document discusses the Eighth Amendment's prohibition against cruel and unusual punishment, referencing **Robinson v. California** and **Powell v. Texas** to illustrate that laws criminalizing a person's status, rather than specific conduct, are unconstitutional. The U.S. argues that criminalizing public sleeping when individuals have no alternative violates this principle [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00003].

The **Jones** case is highlighted as particularly relevant, as it found that enforcement of a similar ordinance was unconstitutional due to inadequate shelter space, effectively criminalizing the status of homelessness. The U.S. urges the court to adopt this reasoning, asserting that punishing individuals for sleeping in public when they have no other options is tantamount to punishing them for being homeless [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00004].

The U.S. concludes that if the court finds that homeless individuals cannot secure shelter due to lack of space or restrictions, then enforcing the anti-camping ordinances would violate the Eighth Amendment. The document emphasizes that criminalizing homelessness does not improve public safety and creates additional barriers for individuals seeking to escape homelessness [CITE:doc_78285_chunk_00006].

In summary, the **Statement of Interest of the United States** advocates for the adoption of the **Jones** framework in evaluating the constitutionality of Boise's anti-camping ordinances, highlighting the need to consider the realities of homelessness and the availability of shelter in the context of Eighth Amendment protections.
